In 2026, New York's observation decks have become art installations, culinary experiences, and immersive worlds designed to make you feel the city in ways you never expected.

The journey started 136 years ago when Gustave Eiffel built the Eiffel Tower for the 1889 World's Fair in Paris. Two million people rushed to see the view in the first six months, proving that people would pay to experience a city from above. New York caught on just one year later, and the Empire State Building's observation deck became a cultural icon, generating $136 million in annual revenue today. But the race to the top has intensified dramatically. In just six years, three major observation decks opened their doors: Edge at Hudson Yards, Summit One Vanderbilt, and others that redefined what it means to view a city.

This year, Hudson Yards invested millions of dollars to transform Edge into the highest outdoor observation deck in the Western Hemisphere. Then there's Pulse, with 450 individually programmed light orbs that respond to human movement. Crystal Cave, a 25-foot canopy of hanging crystals that cast shifting rainbows across the floor. A kaleidoscope where light and color shift in real time. Reflections, with 220 rotating mirrors. Then there's Infinite City, a forest of vertical mirror chambers that makes you feel like you're walking through NYC's skyline while looking at it.

The bet Hudson Yards is making is that New Yorkers and visitors don't just want to see the city from above anymore. They want to feel it refracted, remixed, and reflected back at them. These are living artworks maintained by more than 500 people, designed to create genuine moments of wonder and authentic memories.
