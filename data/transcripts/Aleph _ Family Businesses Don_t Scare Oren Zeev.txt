[SHOW NOTES]
When Oren Zeev first met the founders of Riverside, they were 24 and 20 years old. Brothers. First-time founders. Their product was buggy. They hadn't incorporated a company. And Oren was managing billions of dollars.
He still wrote the check.
Oren — who has also backed a husband-and-wife founding team (Houzz) and has seen plenty of co-founder blow-ups — doesn't believe "family business" is a red flag. His argument: most startups fail anyway. You can invest in two strangers who fight and destroy the company just as easily as two brothers who do. Drawing sweeping conclusions from a single data point is one of the most common mistakes in venture.
He tells the story of a VC who swore off all companies based in Santa Barbara after one bad experience. The founders surfed all day and the company failed. Never again. Oren's reaction: "You're extrapolating from an N of one. Come on."
What he actually looked for in Riverside: five or six new customers paying $40/month, finding the product on their own, with zero marketing spend. That tiny, consistent, organic signal told him everything he needed to know.
